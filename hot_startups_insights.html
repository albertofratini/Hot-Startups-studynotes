01

Oishii

Vertical farming · United States

Indoor strawberry farm using Japanese cultivation methods. Their product — the Omakase Berry — retails at $5 per strawberry.

Luxury logic in a commodity market

By borrowing from the Japanese concept of omakase, Oishii reframed a commodity as an experience. The pricing, the scarcity, the presentation — all borrowed from the luxury goods playbook and dropped into a category that had never seen it. The strawberry became an object of desire rather than a unit of produce.

Controlled environment as a product feature

Indoor vertical farms can dial in light cycles, humidity, CO₂ levels — variables outdoor farming never controls consistently. For flavour-focused products, that reproducibility is the product itself. The strawberry is identical every single time. That predictability is what justifies a premium and enables a direct-to-chef or direct-to-retailer relationship.

Key Takeaway

Repositioning who you're competing with often matters more than competing harder. Oishii opted out of the commodity race and created a different game entirely.

02

Forgis

Industrial AI · Switzerland

A one-stop platform for designing, automating and optimising manufacturing production lines — targeting Western factories being priced out by cheaper overseas labour.

The reindustrialisation tailwind

Western manufacturing has a structural cost problem, and offshoring is increasingly risky — geopolitics, supply chain fragility, ESG pressure are all pushing back. Governments across Europe and the US are actively incentivising domestic production. That's not just a market; it's a customer with a political mandate to spend.

Selling intelligence, not tooling

Earlier waves of manufacturing software sold execution tools — CAD, ERP, MES. Forgis positions as the system that decides, not just the system that executes. That shifts the buyer profile toward C-suite and changes the pricing conversation from seat-based to value-based.

Key Takeaway

Some of the best bets in deep tech right now are boring, critical industries that haven't been touched by modern software and suddenly have a reason to change.

03

Legora

Legal AI · Sweden

Collaborative AI platform built for law firms — handles research, drafting, and document review. Positioned as infrastructure for how legal work gets done, with security and audit trails baked in from day one.

Vertical depth over horizontal reach

General-purpose AI exists. Lawyers use it, then stop, because the output isn't trustworthy enough for actual legal work. Legora's bet is on deep vertical specificity — domain-trained models, citation trails, and compliance architecture — in a market with very high willingness to pay precisely because errors carry real consequences.

Trust as the core product

In legal, being wrong costs clients and bar standing. Speed is a secondary benefit. Legora sells trusted output — a tool lawyers can put their name behind. Every security and audit-trail feature on their site is answering the same question: can I stake my professional reputation on this?

Key Takeaway

In high-stakes professional services, the adoption bar is "I'll stake my reputation on this." Build for that bar.

04

Manufact YC S25

Developer infrastructure · United States

Fullstack SDK to build and deploy MCP apps for ChatGPT and Claude. If a tool you use connects natively to an AI assistant, there's a decent chance Manufact is the plumbing underneath.

Infrastructure compounds quietly

Every major platform wave produces infrastructure companies that end up more durable than the applications built on top — Stripe, Twilio, Segment. MCP is becoming the standard protocol for AI-tool connectivity, and Manufact is building the developer layer before anyone else locks it in. The moat is ecosystem adoption, not code.

Protocol-stage timing

MCP is still early. Most developers haven't heard of it. But the teams building the next generation of AI-native products will need exactly what Manufact offers. Getting in at protocol-formation stage — before competing SDKs proliferate — is the kind of timing that looks obvious later.

Key Takeaway

Watch what protocols are forming, not just what products are launching. The company that owns the plumbing of a new tech stack tends to win quietly and completely.

What ties
these four together

Each one bet on a structural shift, not a trend.

Reindustrialisation, luxury repositioning, AI in professional services, protocol formation — none of these are hype cycles. They're slow-moving structural changes that create durable markets.

The "why now" is unusually clear in each case.

Geopolitics forcing manufacturing home. LLMs making vertical AI viable overnight. MCP becoming a standard. Good startups often succeed because the world moved toward them.

The positioning is defined by what they chose to be, not by the competition.

Oishii is a luxury object. Forgis is industrial intelligence. Legora is trusted output. Manufact is foundational plumbing. All four built an identity that doesn't require a comparison to make sense.
